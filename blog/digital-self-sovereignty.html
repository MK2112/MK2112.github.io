A Systematic Approach to Digital Self-Sovereignty

March 2025 • Reading Time: 10 Minutes

Digital privacy is a perpetual tension between security and usability. Moreover, responding to the question of data ownership has practically evolved into a necessity. To address this need, digital privacy should not be viewed as a binary state but rather as a continuum of choices and probabilistic safeguards. Implemented measures should incrementally reduce a multitude of attack vectors while necessarily getting aligned with one's usability requirements.
I will deconstruct privacy into its components and offer implementable solutions to users seeking to establish meaningful data sovereignty in an increasingly activity-tracking and surveillance-oriented digital ecosystem. If you haven't read Andrej Karpathy's blog post on digital hygiene, I recommend you do so. This post evolved around my two cents on the topic.

Practical Measures

We should adopt a multifaceted approach that addresses both security concerns and usability requirements. Below, we will go into actionable details on data minimization, encryption, anonymization, and consent management.

Password Architecture

Authentication mechanisms are the primary boundary between public and protected data. Contemporary authentication systems continue to rely predominantly on password verification as the initial authentication factor despite well-documented conceptual vulnerabilities. Knowing a password means gaining access. The wide-spread application of passwords necessitates strategic and unique password formulation, which in turn requires strict and confidential management.

KeePassXC offers an interesting solution through offline password storage in an encrypted database file. This approach avoids a fundamental vulnerability inherent in cloud-based password managers: centralized third-party storage inevitably creates distinct, focused attack vectors, regardless of encryption strength or security promises. For instance, users may become more vulnerable to targeted phishing attempts. The logical protections provided by encryption lose their value when trust is excessively placed in a single third party to manage your credentials.

Avoiding cloud-based password managers sounds compelling, but it has usability come into play. What if a user needs to sync passwords across devices after all? In that case, a layered approach can provide a good security-convenience equilibrium. Creating a password database with KeePassXC that is then selectively synchronized via privacy-respecting, general-purpose, peer-to-peer services like SyncThing establishes user ownership while accommodating practical requirements.

Increasingly, passwords are used only as an initial authentication factor. If available, multi-factor authentication (2FA) should universally be enabled. Note that the security benefit of 2FA further increases with physical separation between factors. Therefore, TOTP (Time-based One-Time Password) codes for 2FA should be managed through dedicated, seperate applications, such as the Android app Aegis, rather than being integrated within password managers and right next to the first factor.

Different from TOTP as such, hardware-based authentication devices such as YubiKeys provide superior security through physical factor isolation. TOTP may even be loaded onto a YubiKey, making it the single source for TOTP codes. Note however that multiple YubiKeys should be purchased for distributed backup and the price may preclude adoption.

Biometric authentication should be approached with caution. While it feels secure, unlike passwords, biometric identifiers cannot be revoked or changed following copy or compromise, creating a persistent vulnerability. For your phone, use at least facial recognition and a frequently changed alphanumeric code.

Operating System Architecture & Storage Encryption

Operating system selection significantly impacts privacy posture. Linux distributions (Arch, Debian, CentOS, Linux Mint, etc.) provide transparency through a rigorously open-source architecture and distinctly user-controlled system maintenance. Proprietary systems such as Windows 11 and macOS incorporate increasingly aggressive telemetry and data collection mechanisms. Microsoft's Recall and Copilot functionalities exemplify this trend, requiring manual deactivation and privilege restriction to maintain data sovereignty. You can somewhat achieve this with tools like O&O ShutUp10++. With MacOS, while some data collection can be disabled through the Privacy & Security menu, it is much more challenging to completely block all data sent to Apple's servers.

Full disk encryption is a fundamental privacy requirement across all devices. For optimal security, encryption implementation should be independent from operating system providers when using closed-source systems. VeraCrypt provides a preferred solution for Windows environments, while Linux Unified Key Setup (LUKS) offers native encryption for Linux distributions. Unfortunately there is no widely available alternative to Apple's FileVault for encrypting the boot partition in MacOS.

Web & Network Protection

Browser selection presents a critical privacy decision point. Chromium-based browsers face substantial privacy degradation with the deprecation of the Manifest V2 protocol scheduled for 2024 and 2025. LibreWolf is a Firefox derivative intended for privacy-focused configurations while not being affected by Mozilla's problematic terms of use. Search engine alternatives include Startpage and Searx, which do no track you. If you prefer to stay in the realm of Chromium-based browsers, Ungoogled Chromium with the Chromium Webstore plugin and the uBlock Origin extension is a solid choice. Another open-source Chromium-based alternative is the Brave Browser, which when set to aggressive thoroughly blocks trackers and spoofs the browser's fingerprint.

Network-level content blocking provides superior protection compared to browser-based solutions. DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) services such as RethinkDNS or NextDNS encrypt DNS queries to prevent ISP snooping while providing customizable content filtering across all devices connected to it.

Browser-level protections should be implemented as a secondary layer. For LibreWolf, extensions such as Privacy Badger from the Electronic Frontier Foundation provide solid tracker blocking capabilities. For more protection, script execution control through NoScript (Firefox-based browsers) or uMatrix (Chromium-based browsers) enables granular connection management that yields substantial security benefits over time, albeit with initial configuration complexity. If you don't opt for network-level content blocking, use at least either of the two script execution control extensions and uBlock Origin.

Communication & Productivity

Email is an outdated but deeply established concept. It is inherently insecure by default. Email communication benefits from client-side encryption through applications like Thunderbird, which incorporates PGP support. Email service selection should prioritize jurisdictional considerations, with preference for providers operating under GDPR, Swiss, or Icelandic privacy frameworks.
Use your email only for communication. When registering for services, use email aliases instead. This way, you can easily revoke access to a specific email alias if it gets compromised at any point. A popular choice for email aliases is Addy, which offers a free tier. Some email providers like ProtonMail offer email aliases as well. If you don't want to use aliases, you can use services like 10minutemail.

Document processing should utilize open-source solutions like OnlyOffice or LibreOffice, which avoid data collection and provide increasing compatibility with proprietary document formats.

For messaging, Signal, Element, or Session implement end-to-end encryption with minimized metadata collection. Signal goes as far as to implement post-quantum secure encryption. Activate self-destructing messages. Services such as Telegram, WhatsApp, Discord, and Meta's platforms should be avoided or used at most with carefully configured privacy settings.

Social media engagement and exposure should be minimized aswell. When necessary, consumption without authentication prevents cross-platform tracking. Authentication via social media credentials for third-party services creates extensive tracking vectors and should be categorically avoided.

Cloud Services & Network Routing

General purpose cloud storage selection should prioritize services with robust privacy frameworks (GDPR, Swiss/Icelandic law) and ISO 27001 certification. Proton Drive and pCloud represent services meeting these criteria. However, if possible, one should resort to peer-to-peer solutions like SyncThing to exclusively transfer data directly between your devices.

Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) require careful evaluation, as they necessarily route all network traffic through their infrastructure. Mullvad has demonstrated consistent and thorough privacy practices. Most other providers make their VPN service a black box or they either maintain at least certain logging practices or actively monitor traffic. The architectural reality of VPNs creates an inherent trust requirement one should constantly be aware of.

Mobile Platform Hardening (Android)

Android's architecture permits users to actually apply privacy enhancements themselves. I'll split this into three parts: First, we'll look at how to make the best out of your current Android. Then, we'll takt this up a notch and see what advanced users may want to do to further tweak their existing Android systems. Finally, we'll look at the best hollistic solution to Android privacy, hardware- and software-wise.
For immediate application, one should carefully configure the Android privacy settings. Removing the Android System SafetyCore app and replacing the default GBoard keyboard with privacy-respecting alternatives like the FUTO Keyboard reduces additional data collection and tracking vectors. More generally, Android users should consider open-source app alternatives from sources like F-Droid. Need a QR code scanner? Go look on F-Droid first, you will most likely find a good alternative there that isn't data hungry by design. For browsing, privacy-preserving options like the Firefox-based Fennec or the Chromium-based Brave Browser should be considered.

Advanced users can implement Shizuku and Canta for deeper system modification, enabling removal of proprietary system applications. This approach requires careful research to prevent system instability.

Note that if you have a choice, Google Pixel phones are uniquely suited for installing GrapheneOS, a privacy-focused Android fork. GrapheneOS achieves enhanced privacy by stripping away Google service integrations while providing extensive control over individual apps, their permissions, network access, and background activities overall. Additionally, a hardened memory management is intended to protect against potential app vulnerabilities. GrapheneOS, while not that trivial to install, provides a significantly more secure experience than stock Android or iOS.

Artificial Intelligence & Large Language Models

AI interaction presents emerging privacy challenges. Locality and data ownership represent optimal approaches, with tools like Ollama enabling local model operation. When utilizing web-based or cloud-based Large Language Models (LLMs), providers with established privacy frameworks such as OpenAI or Anthropic should be selected, although even these should be treated with caution.

All non-local LLM interfaces should be treated as potential keystroke loggers. Personally identifiable information, confidential data, and sensitive content should never be shared with non-local models.

The Connected Identity

Privacy discourse often focuses on data access prevention while neglecting the increasing identity contextualization. Modern tracking extends beyond traditional cookies to behavioral fingerprinting that creates persistent cross-platform identifiers. As shown in a post from Proton, these techniques pose a USP for data brokers as they enable comprehensive profile construction for even more targeted ads regardless of individual privacy measures.

While certain tools claim to help and to find and delete your data from data brokers, they are not solving the problem. Research suggests that many anti-fingerprinting techniques may not be as effective as claimed and can sometimes make users even more identifiable due to the inconsistencies the measures may introduce. Still, some measures can be applied.
For Firefox-based browsers, you can follow the concept of uniformity: Make your browser look distinctly like everybody else's. This is achieved by enabling the privacy.resistFingerprinting flag in about:config, which LibreWolf has enabled by default.

You can add to the concept of uniformity the idea of randomness within bounds that still suggest normality. You may want to install the User-Agent Switcher and Manager and have your browser pretend to be Google Chrome, the most commonly used browser. You should also setup CanvasBlocker to actively prevent websites from using certain Javascript APIs for fingerprinting.

GDPR: Tech Bro's Nightmare

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) represents the most comprehensive privacy legislation implemented globally. Despite criticism from technology executives, particularly those with business models dependent on aspects of surveillance capitalism, GDPR fundamentally rebalances power dynamics between users and technology providers by establishing data ownership principles.

GDPR's core provisions include explicit consent requirements, data minimization, purpose limitation, accountability mechanisms, and the right to complete erasure. These requirements are legally binding with substantial penalties reaching 4% of global annual revenue or €20 million, exemplified by Meta's €390 million fine issued in January 2023.
GDPR's extraterritorial application requires compliance from any entity processing EU citizens' data, generating global impact. When users can demand transparency regarding data collection and algorithmic decision-making, business models predicated on opaque data extraction become unsustainable.

Opposition to GDPR reveals the uncomfortable reality that many "innovative" business models depend fundamentally on privacy asymmetries rather than foremost genuine value creation. Companies claiming innovation requires unrestricted data access are acknowledging their inability to obtain informed consent for their practices.
Claims that GDPR inhibits innovation are creating popularity for said opposition. They invert reality. European companies effectively enjoy competitive advantages in global markets as similar regulations start to proliferate. Companies including Mullvad and Proton have developed successful business models with GDPR compliance as a differentiating feature of quality and an indicator for trustworthiness when it comes to privacy.

Conclusion: Probabilistic Protection

Comprehensive privacy requires systematic backups with encryption, geographic distribution, and regular updates. Privacy fundamentally represents a probabilistic challenge rather than a binary state - each implemented protection layer compounds security benefits while reducing compromise probability.

The privacy framework presented here balances theoretical ideals with practical implementation constraints. While some usability friction is unavoidable, security benefits can substantially outweigh convenience costs.
The objective is not paranoid isolation but intentional data sovereignty. Users should maintain awareness of data flows, implement reasonable protective measures, and exercise conscious decision-making regarding digital engagement. Privacy represents not merely a technical challenge but a fundamental component of digital agency.
